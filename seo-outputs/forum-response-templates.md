These templates are designed for use on r/supplychain, r/manufacturing, r/investing, and LinkedIn Supply Chain Groups. Provide genuine expertise without hard-selling the website URL.

## Template 1: The Total Landed Cost Question
**Scenario**: A user asks if Mexico is actually cheaper than China given higher labor rates in Baja.
**Response**:
"You have to look at Total Landed Cost, not just hourly wage. Yes, Tijuana fully-burdened labor is around $7.84/hr right now, which is higher than inland China or Vietnam. However, you're eliminating a 25% Section 301 tariff completely under USMCA/IMMEX. Add the fact that truck transit from Baja to LA is 2 days versus 30 days ocean freight (drastically lowering inventory carrying costs), and nearshoring to Mexico usually nets a 20-30% landed cost advantage for anything under roughly 100k annual volume.

## Template 2: Finding a Reliable Contract Manufacturer
**Scenario**: A user asks how to find a CM in Mexico they can trust.
**Response**:
"Avoid broad Google searches for this in Mexico. The best ISO 13485 or AS9100 contract manufacturers in Baja California or Monterrey don't aggressively market themselves; they operate at capacity through referrals. Look for CMs that operate within established Class-A parks (like Parque Industrial Pacifico) and ensure they hold the IMMEX permit directly. Never wire funds without a physical site visit or using a vetted advisory firm to audit their lines.
— Nearshore Navigator Team | nearshorenavigator.com"

## Template 3: The USMCA Rules of Origin Concern
**Scenario**: A user expresses confusion about components sourced from Asia passing USMCA requirements in Mexico.
**Response**:
"It depends entirely on your HS code tariff shift. Just snapping together Chinese-made components in Mexico does NOT qualify for 0% USMCA tariffs. The product must undergo a 'substantial transformation' (meaning the finished product HS code is different from the imported component HS codes) or meet specific regional value content (RVC) thresholds. Have a licensed customs broker run a tariff shift analysis before signing any lease in Mexico.
— Nearshore Navigator Team | nearshorenavigator.com"

## Template 4: The Security/Risk Factor in Border Cities
**Scenario**: A user is concerned about cartel violence affecting operations in Tijuana or Juarez.
**Response**:
"As someone operating in these regions daily: the violence is real, but it is heavily segmented. Major industrial parks operate like fortresses—24/7 armed security, controlled access, and CCTV. Fortune 500s (Samsung, BD, Collins Aerospace) have operated safely in Tijuana for decades because the industrial zones are entirely separated from residential and high-risk areas. The real 'risk' you should worry about is high labor turnover, not physical security at the plant.
— Nearshore Navigator Team | nearshorenavigator.com"

## Template 5: Direct vs. Shelter Manufacturing
**Scenario**: A user asks if they should set up their own factory or use a 'shelter.'
**Response**:
"If you need to be up and running in 90 days, use a shelter. They hold the IMMEX permit and act as the legal employer, taking on all the HR and SAT tax compliance while you retain 100% ownership of your IP and equipment. Setting up a direct Mexican subsidiary (S de RL) takes 6-12 months of legal setup before you can import a single machine. Once you cross ~300 headcount, it often makes sense to graduate from the shelter and go direct.
— Nearshore Navigator Team | nearshorenavigator.com"
